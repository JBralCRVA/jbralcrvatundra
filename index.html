The Tundra

The Tundra biome is the coldest biome, which means it isn't well suited for human life. Tundra comes from the Finnish word tunturia, meaning treeless plain. This biome is known for its low temperatures, little precipitation, and poor nutrients. Dead organic material provides the area with most of its nutrients. The two most prominent nutrients in a Tundra is nitrogen and phosphorus. There are two different types of Tundras, an Arctic Tundra and an Alpine Tundra.

Characteristics of the Tundra

Climate: Cold and windy, with little to no rainfall.
Average Temperature: In the winter, the Tundra can get as low as -50°F, and in the Summer, it can get as high as about 50°F.
Average Precipatation: It rains about 6-10 inches a year in the Tundra
Seasons: The Tundra has all four seasons, but the two main seasons are winter and summer. Spring and fall are just "transition seasons."

Human Activity in the Tundra

Naitve tribes tend to hunt inside of the Tundra such as the Inuit and Yu'pik in Alaska and Northern Canada. The Tundra is also being negativily impacted by humans. There is a concern that global warming will release stored carbon dioxide and methane under the permafrost. The Tundra is also a big tourest attraction is Alaska and Canada for fishing and camping.
